# Ch. 1 — Awakening at Number One, London

---
time: "Friday, November 2, 1917, 17:36"
place: 51.50359389949132, -0.15104752054828927
---

"Relay set up complete."

Chadt blinked, re-read the words. He did not feel any different. But he was also certain that something had changed.

His eyes darted through the page.

*London. 1917.*

Just two words. But the words painted pictures.

A river. Grey and wide. Bridges strung with fog. The silhouette of a dome through the smog. *He had no reference, no memory, but the city unfolded in his skull like a map being opened — streets, squares, the curve of the Thames, the black thread of a railway cutting through stone. He knew none of it. He saw all of it.*

*November 2nd. Friday evening.*

The cold came first. A damp, creeping chill that settled in his bones before he could name it. He shivered, but his body didn't respond right — the shiver was a memory of a shiver, a ghost of sensation on skin that wasn't quite there.

*The heart of the Empire. A city at war.*

He heard it. Far off, low, a droning hum. Engines. Then the whistle — thin at first, building. An explosion that didn't reach his ears but hit his chest, a thump that knocked the air out of him. The screams came after, muffled, like hearing a nightmare from the other side of a wall.

Chadt's hands trembled on the screen. He tried to look away, but the words held his gaze.

*A library. Somewhere in Mayfair. 148 Piccadilly.*

He blinked and the room was *there*, at the edges of his vision, like a photograph developing. High ceilings. Mahogany. Shelves of leather-bound books that stretched into shadow. A fire crackling in a hearth he couldn't see — he *heard* it, the pop and hiss of green wood, and then he *smelled* it, woodsmoke threading through the air of his room, displacing the stale breath of his bedroom.

He looked up from the screen.

The walls of his room were still there. But they were *wrong*. The shadows flickered at a different rate than his lamp. The air had weight he didn't recognize. The smoke — he could taste it now, old paper and coal dust and the faint metallic tang of a city burning.

He looked back at the page.

*A desk. Massive. Dark wood carved with ornaments that bled into each other — a unicorn, a lion, a dragon with too many teeth, a bundle of bloodied arrows.*

The details slipped away as he tried to grasp them. But he *saw* them. A desk the size of a door. A surface covered in fractal patterns that grew more intricate the longer he stared.

*On top of the desk, a letter. In a pool of blood.*

The blood moved. Shifted. Bubbled like it was alive, absorbing into paper that stayed impossibly white beneath it. The red was too red. The white too white. And the letter *called* to him, a pull in his chest that felt like vertigo.

Chadt's hand moved toward the screen. He didn't tell it to.

No — not toward the screen. His hand was already past it. Reaching for something he couldn't see. The room around him was *gone*. The desk was in front of him, real and solid. The fire was to his left, casting shadows that danced across the books. The floor under his bare feet was carpet, thick and old and woven with patterns that whispered when he shifted his weight.

He was in the library. He was *here*.

"Human. You are in the Imaginary Plane."

The voice came from nowhere and everywhere. Crisp. Clear. Like a voice that had never been used before.

Chadt spun, looking for the source. The books around him jumped at the sudden movement — not the books themselves, but the space *around* them, rippling like a pond disturbed.

"Do not attempt to recall your arrival or it could destabilize you. Focus on the present. Smell the firewood."

"What is this? Where am I? Who are you?"

He tried to form the words aloud, but his mouth didn't obey. The thought was enough.

"I am Tian. You are in the Imaginary Plane. You read a relay. The relay brought you here. I will guide you through the operation."

"I didn't ask for this. Make it stop. Wake me up."

"You are not sleeping. There is nothing to wake from. The letter on the desk — take it. That is your way out."

"Take the — what? No. I want out. I want to go back."

"You cannot go back the way you came. The relay is one-way. But you can go *forward*. Take the letter. Follow its thread. When the thread reaches its end, you return."

Chadt's heart hammered. The books around him trembled at the rhythm, their spines vibrating, pages rustling like wings.

"Calm down," said the voice. "You are feeding the instability. Rub your hands together. Feel the friction."

He didn't want to follow instructions from a voice in his head. But he also didn't want the world to fall apart. He pressed his palms together. The skin was cold, distant, like touching someone else's hands.

"Stabilization sequence complete. Short-term coherence established."

The trembling slowed. Chadt's feet were on solid ground — a ground made of giant pages, covered in script that swarmed like insects. He tried not to look at it too long.

"Do not exert force. This space responds to intent."

The voice paused. When it continued, it was gentler.

"Humans like to be addressed by names, right? What do I call you?"

*Chadt.* The word surfaced before he could second-guess it. His name. The one thing he was sure of.

"Chadt." The voice pronounced the silent *t* — a sound he'd never heard from anyone's mouth before.

The ring of books around him was now almost still. Chadt tried to touch one of the spines. His fingers passed through it and he fell.

"...a bit early for that," said the voice. "Let me help. Count your breaths."

Chadt inhaled slowly, though no air filled his lungs. Up became up and down became down.

Chadt exhaled, and the books shrank to fill endless shelves slithering around him.

With the next breath, he had a full body.

One more and the world relaxed into place.

The library was real now. The desk was in front of him. The letter was on it, in a pool of blood that bubbled softly, absorbing into paper that never stained.

"That's the letter you were talking about."

"Yes. Take it. That's your way forward."

Chadt hesitated. The letter terrified him. Not the blood — that was wrong, but it was the *wrongness* of the paper that got him. The way it stayed white. The way the ink looked too old, too black, like it had been written in a language that predated meaning.

"What happens when I take it?"

"The thread appears. You follow it. I guide you. When it ends, you go home."

"That's it?"

"That's not *it*. But that's the shape of it."

Chadt reached out. His hand passed through the air like it was thick water. His fingers touched the envelope.

Everything was wrong. The envelope was too heavy. The white was too white. The ink too old. And beneath the confusion, a flicker of something vast — *loss*. Like an echo of lives stolen, pressing against his fingers. An open wound he yearned to understand. A sense of kinship he couldn't place.

He picked it up.

A thin shimmery thread extended from the envelope, pointing toward an oversized panelled door at the far end of the room. He hadn't noticed it before.

Behind him, a service door creaked open.
