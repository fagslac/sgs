# Ch. 1 — Awakening at Number One

---
time: "Friday, November 2, 1917, 17:36"
place: 51.50359389949132, -0.15104752054828927
---




"Relay set up complete."

Chadt blinked, re-read the words. He did not feel any different. But he was also certain that something had changed.

His eyes darted through the page. London. 1917.

And then suddenly, he was there.

The walls of his room vanished as his imagination took over.

The words painted pictures, played sounds, triggered smells.

Massive zeppelins were dropping bombs. The screams of the people turned to a sour, burnt taste in his mouth. 

Memories, dreams and illusions blended in a whirlwind of wet fire and dry soot.

"Do not panic. Focus on my voice."

The voice was an anchor, but the storm didn't care. Chadt felt himself *falling* — not down, but outward. Through everything the words had promised. A street of mud and cobblestones rushed toward him. He passed *through* a horse mid-gallop, its ribs visible through translucent skin. A woman's face loomed close, mouth open in a silent wail, then dissolved into ash that tasted of ink. Fingers — his? not his? — scrabbled at wet brick as the ground twisted into a ceiling and back into a floor.

"Stop trying to see. You're making it worse."

The words themselves were hooks. Every sentence he'd read was buried in his skull, unpacking itself, constructing a world from the inside out. He was the reader. He was the page. He was the space between the letters where the meaning leaked through.

"Do not fight the current. Let it carry you."

Chadt stopped thrashing. The falling became floating. The fragments cohered into a blur of grey and brown — a city, a river, a bridge strung with fog. He was moving *toward* something. Or something was pulling him.

A library. The shape of it resolved out of the chaos like a photograph developing. High ceilings. Rows of leather-bound books. The smell of woodsmoke and old paper. A fire crackling somewhere he couldn't see.

And a voice — the same voice, but closer now. Warmer.

"Stabilization sequence complete. Short-term coherence established."

The world snapped into focus around him.










Chadt was standing in a vast circular room lined with books that stretched up into shadows. The spines bore golden crests — a unicorn, a lion, a dragon with too many teeth, a bundle of bloodied arrows. The crests shifted when he blinked, their details rearranging themselves just out of comprehension. The ground under his feet was made of giant pages, covered in script that swarmed like insects.

"Is this real?" The words came out as a thought, but he felt them land.





"Real enough," said the voice. "You are in the Imaginary Plane. The words you read were a relay. I am Tian. I will guide you."

"What is this place? How do I make it stop?"

"You don't. Not yet. But I can help you stabilize if you let me."

"Just tell me how to get out."

"You will return when the operation is complete. For now, let me ground you. Look at your hands."

Chadt looked down. He had hands — he thought. They flickered at the edges, like a badly rendered shape.

"Rub them together. Feel the friction."

He followed the instruction. His palms against each other felt alien and not quite right. But the contact helped. A fixed point in the sensory storm. The books around him, which had been trembling at the edge of his vision, slowed their dance.

"Better. The Imaginary Plane responds to intent. Do not exert force. Do not try to control. Just *be* here."

Chadt took a breath. No air filled his lungs, but the rhythm steadied him. Up became up. Down became down.

"Count your breaths."

He inhaled. The books settled into rows.

He exhaled. The shelves slid into place, forming a corridor of leather and gold.

With the next breath, he had a full body.

One more and the world relaxed into place.

The room was an old library. Mahogany and marble and the distant hush of dust settling. In front of him, a massive desk carved with fractal ornaments. On its surface, an envelope.

No. An envelope in a pool of blood.

The red was too bright, too *active* — it shifted and bubbled like it was alive, and the paper stayed impossibly white beneath it, absorbing nothing. Chadt's stomach dropped. The letter called to him with a gravity that felt physical.

"What *is* that thing?"

"A letter. Take it, and we can proceed."

Chadt shook his head. "I'm not touching that. I don't know what this is, I don't know who you are, I don't know —"

"I am Tian. I am the voice that pulled you out of the storm. The letter is a Leak. A wound in reality. We are here to follow it to its source and close it."

"We? I didn't agree to —"

"You read the spell. You are here. That is agreement enough for now."

Chadt wanted to argue, but a sound stopped him. A creak. Wood on wood. From the far end of the room, a bookshelf was shifting — reshaping itself into a service door. It was opening. Slowly. Inevitably.

"Oh dear," said Tian.

Shelves began to tremble. Books fell to the ground.

"I know you have questions. But we need to move. Take the letter, go through the main door, and we will talk on the other side."

"What is that thing?"

"The Housemaids. They maintain the Imaginary Plane. They do not appreciate intruders. And you — we — are intruders. Take the letter. Now."

Chadt looked at the envelope. The blood bubbled. The paper gleamed. Every instinct told him to run *away* from it.

But the door was opening. And he could see, just barely, the glint of metallic fingers curling around its edge. Too many of them. Moving with the precision of clockwork.

He grabbed the envelope.

Everything was wrong. The envelope was too heavy. The white was too white. The ink too old. And beneath the confusion, a flicker of something vast — *loss*. Like an echo of stolen lives pressing against his fingers. An open wound that yearned to be understood.

Chadt shivered and pushed the feeling down.

"Good. Now the main door. Quickly."

A thin shimmery thread extended from the letter, pointing toward an oversized panelled door. Chadt ran—or tried to. His legs were rubber, his balance uncertain. Behind him, the service door groaned open another inch.

"Forget your body. Just think about moving. Imagine yourself gliding."

Chadt imagined. His body lurched forward, then smoothed into something like a stumble, then a jog. He reached the panelled door just as the clockwork fingers tapped the frame behind him.

He pushed. The door swung open into darkness, and he ran through.

The room beyond was a space that refused to be contained. White marble veined with gold. Chandeliers multiplied into infinity. On the walls, thousands of identical portraits stared straight at him. A dry cold sucked the air from his lungs.

The next breath was thick. A translucent miasma filled the room, coiling into fleeting hands, smiles, eyes. It tasted faintly of brass.

"What is that?"

"The Hall Boys," said Tian, matter-of-fact. "The General Errand Field. The invisible skeleton of the household. Just pretend not to see them."

Chadt swallowed his protests and moved. Empty fingers brushed his skin. Faint whispers tickled his ears. He kept walking.

The far doors were stark-edged and towering. Behind him, the clockwork fingers had reached the threshold. He did not look back.

He pushed through the final doors and found himself on a street. Grey. Muffled. A layer of soot covered everything — pedestrians frozen mid-stride, carriages suspended in time, a motorcar caught in a cloud of exhaust. The world was a photograph of itself, drained and silent.

"You may now relax."

Tian's voice held a warmth that Chadt hadn't noticed until now, when the pressure eased.

"Relax? I just — what the hell was that?"

"Your first transition. You handled it well, for a baseline human."

Chadt stared at the frozen world. The letter was still in his hand, impossibly bright against the grey. A thin thread of light trailed from it, disappearing into the soot.

"I believe you have some explaining to do."

"You're right. I definitely do." Tian's tone shifted — humble, almost apologetic. "Let me make it up to you. I know the perfect place."
