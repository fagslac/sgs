
"Just like that, very good."

The voice was steady. Calm. Chadt wanted to hate it, but the hatred wouldn't come. The curiosity had cracked something open, and the crack was letting light in.

"Let me out," he said. Or thought. He wasn't sure anymore.

"The only exit is forward."

"Forward to *what*?"

"To the source of the thread you're holding. A library. The Rothschild library at 148 Piccadilly. You need to see it."

"I don't see anything. It's all dark."

"Look closer."

Chadt strained. The darkness didn't change. But then, slowly — a line where there was no line before. A corner. A shadow that had weight. The outline of something large and solid.

"What is that?"

"You're building it. Keep looking."

He looked. The shape grew. Tall shelves, stacked with books. A desk. A hearth. The image came in pieces, each one joining the last like a puzzle solving itself. But the pieces didn't fit right. The ceiling slanted where it shouldn't. The shelves leaned. The books were too big and too small at the same time, flickering between sizes like a badly tuned signal.

"What's wrong with it?"

"Nothing. You're doing fine. You've never built a room from scratch before. The instability is normal."

"I'm doing this?"

"You are in the Imaginary Plane. Here, intent shapes reality. Your attention builds the world. Unfortunately, your attention is also *afraid*, so the world is coming out a little..."

A shelf bulged outward. The books on it multiplied, then halved, then swarmed like startled birds before snapping back into place.

"...unstable."

"How do I fix it?"

"You don't fix. You settle. Rub your hands together. Feel the friction."

Chadt brought his palms together. The contact was rough, warm. Alien. Like shaking a stranger's hand. But it was *something*.

"The room responds to your nervous system. Your heart is beating too fast. The books can hear it."

He looked around. The shelves were trembling — not from an earthquake, but from his *pulse*. The rhythm of his fear was the rhythm of the world.

"Count your breaths. Slow them down."

Chadt inhaled. The trembling eased.

Another breath. The ceiling straightened.

A third. The shelves slid into place.

"Stabilization sequence complete. Short-term coherence established."

Chadt looked down. He had feet. They were standing on a ground made of pages — giant sheets covered in script that crawled like insects. He tried not to stare too long.

"Do not exert force. This space responds to intent. The more you push, the more it pushes back."

He swallowed. Or tried to. His throat didn't quite cooperate.

"Who are you? What is this place?"

"I am Tian. I will guide you through your first operation in the Imaginary Plane. The Imaginary Plane is the space between minds — where attention becomes architecture. Yours is not the first consciousness to walk here, but it is the most *alive* we've had in a long time."

"You can hear my thoughts?"

"To some degree. You are very exposed right now. When you settle, I will only hear what you intend to say."

"And when I get out of here?"

"When the operation is complete. You take the letter. You follow its thread to the source. You seal the Leak. Then you return."

"The letter. That thing on the desk."

"Yes."

Chadt looked at the letter. White. Too white. Resting in a pool of blood that bubbled without ever touching it. He looked away.

"Why me?"

"That is a long answer. For now — you read the spell. You were available. You were compatible."

"Available."

"Yes."

The word hung. Chadt should have been furious. Instead he found himself studying the shelves, the hearth, the impossible geometry of a room that existed only because he was looking at it. The books were gigantic. Their spines bore crests that shifted when he blinked — a unicorn, a lion, a dragon with too many teeth, a bundle of bloodied arrows.

"What are those?"

"Family crests. The Rothschilds collected them. They also collected books. This library holds some of the rarest volumes in the world. In your world, anyway. Here it holds whatever you need it to hold."

"But I've never seen this place before."

"You don't need to have seen it. You need to *imagine* it. I am helping."

The voice paused. When it continued, it was almost gentle.

"Humans like to be addressed by names, right? What do I call you?"

*Chadt.* The word surfaced before he could second-guess it. His name. The one thing he was sure of in this place.

"Chadt." The voice pronounced the silent *t*.

No one had ever done that before. Not his parents. Not his teachers. Not even the kids who bothered to say his name at all. The *t* had always been a ghost — present in spelling, absent in speech. But this voice found it and held it.

*Chadt.* Like it mattered how it was said.

Something in him unclenched. Barely. But enough.

"Do not attempt to recall your arrival or it could destabilize you," said Tian. "Focus on the present. Smell the firewood, would you?"

He breathed in. Woodsmoke. Old paper. The faint metallic tang of the blood on the desk. The room was still now, the shelves settled, the giant books waiting.

The ring of books around him was almost still. Chadt tried to touch one of the spines, but his fingers passed through it and he fell.
