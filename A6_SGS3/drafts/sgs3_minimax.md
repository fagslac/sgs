# 3 - The Royal Cock Pit

The walk from the Athenaeum to Downing Street was eerily quiet. Chadt and Tian strolled down Horse Guards Road, St James's Park spreading its sickly foliage to their right. The war had depleted its population. Ducks waddled lone paths, their lamentations the only sound in the cold silence.

Chadt could almost forget it was not real. Almost. His mind wouldn't stop swirling with questions that kept pouring even as Tian's strange beauty demanded his full attention. The angel walked beside him, feet hovering slightly above the ground, blue hair catching light that had no source.

"If you're from the future," Chadt said, the words tumbling out before he could second-guess them, "do you know everything that will happen?"

Tian's lean legs went slack for a moment. The body kept sliding along as if nothing had happened, but the pause stretched.

"I can search our archives," Tian replied carefully, "but they're rather empty. Most historical records were lost during the Singularity. That's precisely why we're doing this."

The envelope in Chadt's hand seemed to grow heavier with each step. He stared at it — that impossibly white rectangle, dripping nothing now but menace.

For a fraction of a second, everything shifted.

The pond in the park simmered with thick blood. It climbed the grass and washed over white stone buildings. A cold, metallic smell filled Chadt's lungs. In the middle of that crimson ocean, a massive cube of black brick throbbed with a deep, impossible rhythm.

Then it passed. The image vanished, replaced by the grim reality of Number 10 Downing Street — the building he'd seen on the news, the beating heart of British power. The iconic black door was crowned by an iridescent lantern. Fences sliced the cold air into solemn strips.

Chadt shrank under the oppressive aura radiating from the dark stone.

"It's supposed to be yellow," Tian interjected.

Chadt blinked. Turned. The angel's wavy hair framed a pale, grinning face.

"Everybody forgot about it. The smog turned it black. Then they kept it that way."

"Aren't you scared?" Chadt pressed, disbelief sharpening his voice. "Isn't this the best protected place in the world?"

Tian paused before answering.

"Maybe in the Real Plane. In the Imaginary, it's one of the most famous places. That means it's easy to map. The Royal Cock Pit is basically a rite of passage for every student of my department."

"The Royal cockpit?" Chadt asked with a sneer, grasping for humor to cut the tension. "Because that's where they pilot the country?"

"No, a pit with actual cocks." Tian gestured toward the right, where a trail of light followed. "Henry VIII had an arena for cockfighting here, with lodgings next to it. It became a theater for a while, then the seat of government. The cockfighting never really stopped though."

Tian snickered. Chadt didn't. The angel studied him with inquisitive eyes.

"Did I do humor wrong?"

"I'm a little distracted because you're sending me into the last dungeon of the British Empire."

"Oh please, this is barely the starting zone," replied the angel with clearly unearned confidence.

The wind howled with screams that might have been millions. The walls were so polished they were almost blinding. Chadt shuddered.

"So how do we get in?"

"Actually, during most of its existence, this place was relatively poorly protected." Tian gestured toward a titanesque silhouette that Chadt had first thought was a statue, due to its surhuman proportions. The guard was human, but rendered monstrous by his stillness — so sedentarized he was barely a threat. Yet Chadt couldn't shake the feeling something was off.

"But since we'll be bypassing them, we'll only see staff and ministers. Much too busy with their own chores and their own selves to pay us any mind."

"You're blue and sparkling," Chadt pointed out.

"And you really underestimate how self-involved British gentlemen can get." Tian's smile didn't waver. "Still, you're right to be cautious."

The angel dimmed the colors of both their clothes to a dim, dark shade of grey. They looked almost transparent.

"Here. The Camouflage of the Unremarkable. As long as you act like you belong, no one will question you."

"Belong," Chadt repeated flatly. "Right."

"Of course, it's always best not to linger." Tian's voice dropped. "They might be signing it already."

Chadt's stomach clenched. The letter. The Payload. Arthur Balfour's letter that would commit a crime so vast it echoed across a century.

"Wait." Chadt grabbed for Tian's arm, but his hand passed through like smoke. Tian stopped anyway.

"So we go in, wait for the moment they sign and do the ritual, right?" Chadt asked. "What's our way out?"

Tian seemed surprised by the question.

"I thought you'd just wake up."

Chadt didn't know what to object to in that response, so he said nothing.

They approached Number 11 — condemned when the houses were joined, Tian explained, but here a door was a door. The angel led them through into what looked like a narrow hallway, then the space began to stretch and breathe like a living thing.

The entrance hall opened into a corridor lined with portraits — faces Chadt didn't recognize, eyes that followed him just slightly too closely. The dimensions refused to settle. One moment the ceiling was low enough to touch; the next it vaulted into shadow.

"This place feels different from the Rothschild manor," Chadt observed, keeping his voice low.

"It is different." Tian's tone carried an edge Chadt hadn't heard before. "We're not here to take anything. We're here to witness. The Imaginary responds differently to observers than to thieves."

Chadt thought of the Housemaids — those clockwork creatures that had chased him in the library. He'd seen them in the corner of his vision since entering Downing Street, but every time he turned to look, they vanished.

"The Housemaids are here?" he whispered.

"Every household has them. They're the skeleton. The unseen labor that keeps everything running." Tian didn't break stride. "Don't worry. They don't see us either. Not unless we make trouble."

They passed through a series of antichambers that seemed to multiply as they walked — an ante-room, then another, then a small parlor with faded velvet chairs. A maid crossed their path, her eyes fixed straight ahead, not registering their presence at all.

The dream-logic of it made Chadt's head swim. He focused on his breathing, on the feel of the envelope in his hand, on Tian's presence beside him.

They arrived at a door at the back of the house — the Cabinet Room. Through it came the murmur of voices, the low rumble of argument.

"Ready?" Tian asked.

Chadt was not ready. He said nothing and followed Tian through the door.

The room was smaller than he'd expected. A long table dominated the space, surrounded by seven men in suits, their faces tight with exhaustion and conviction. Maps covered the walls. A fire crackled in the grate, doing little to warm the chill of history.

At the head of the table sat a stocky Welshman with a shock of dark hair — David Lloyd George, the Prime Minister, his eyes sharp as broken glass. He wasn't speaking now, but his silence had weight.

But it was the man at the table's far end who commanded Chadt's attention.

Arthur Balfour was tall, angular, with a pale face and thin lips that seemed perpetually amused by a joke no one else understood. His eyes were pale blue, almost colorless, and when they moved across the room, they seemed to look through things rather than at them.

One gentleman stood — Curzon, Chadt guessed, from Tian's earlier warnings. He was arguing with evident vehemence, his voice rising.

"That's Georges Curzon," Tian murmured, leaning close. His breath was cool against Chadt's ear. "He's the main opponent. They'll beat him before long."

Curzon towered over his seat, hands flat on the table.

**"You've never been there, Lord Balfour, but I have, many times."** Curzon's voice was thunderous, almost desperate. **"I'm telling you, this land can't sustain more souls. You've seen the rainfall tables. The crop yields. The water tables. It's arithmetic, not opinion."**

Balfour didn't rise to match him. The Foreign Secretary remained seated, almost lazily composed.

**"It won't have to for long, George."** Balfour's voice was soft, almost gentle — the voice of a man who had never once doubted himself. **"Soon enough, measly secular land won't be a consideration. We're already leading the studies into the beyond. We're playing a much longer game. This is about the salvation of souls."**

Chadt felt the air in the room thicken. Something shifted — a pressure behind his eyes, a taste of brass.

**"You're promising someone else's home!"** Curzon slammed his palm on the table. **"You're promising Palestine to the Zionists without consulting a single Palestinian! You're promising land you've never set foot on to people who've never lived there, while the people who've lived there for centuries get — what? Your benevolence?"**

Balfour's pale eyes flickered.

**"They're an inferior race, George. Surely you've read the research."** The smile was infinitesimal, terrible. **"This isn't a matter of opinion. It's a matter of historical necessity. The weak point of our position, of course, is that in the case of Palestine we deliberately and rightly decline to accept the principle of self-determination. We do not propose even to go through the form of consulting the wishes of the present inhabitants."**

Chadt's hands trembled. The letter in his grip seemed to pulse with a terrible heat. He wanted to shout, to intervene, to do something —

He felt Tian's hand on his arm. The touch was cool, firm.

*Don't*, the angel's eyes said. *We can't do anything. We must not waste our chance to witness.*

Chadt's jaw tightened until his teeth ached.

Curzon wasn't done.

**"You're putting yolks on people you've never even consulted!"** The words came faster now, desperate, the voice of a man watching disaster approach and unable to stop it. **"You're making blood their only option! You're planting mines on holy sites, and when they blow — when they blow, Balfour — you'll have nothing but corpses!"**

Balfour's smile didn't waver.

**"We need them out of here, George. It's very simple."** He spread his hands, a professor explaining an obvious theorem. **"The presence of Jews in Europe is a problem. The presence of Arabs in Palestine is a problem. One of these problems is solvable with a letter. We are solving it."**

Chadt watched Curzon deflate. The man's shoulders dropped. His hands fell from the table.

And then Curzon tried one last time.

**"We already gave our word!"** The cry was raw now, almost pleading. **"The McMahon-Hussein correspondence! We promised the Arabs an independent kingdom! We've already committed!"**

Balfour rose for the first time. He was very tall.

**"We never agreed to any details, George."** The voice was almost kind. **"The correspondence left ample provisions for future leverage. We demanded exception clauses specifically for this moment. I'm afraid you've been outmaneuvered."**

Curzon stared at him. The silence in the room was absolute.

Chadt looked at Curzon — at this man who had fought with facts, with documents, with the weight of the world as it was — and saw the moment he understood he had already lost. The wall behind him seemed to crack, a lattice of limestone and grief.

The Prime Minister cleared his throat.

"I agree with Arthur," David Lloyd George said.

Three words. The procedural authority of the office. The fight was over before it had technically begun.

Chadt felt something twist in his chest — grief, or rage, or both. Tian's grip on his arm tightened.

*Now*, the angel's eyes said. *The ritual. Now or never.*

Balfour was reaching for a piece of paper. The paper.

This was the moment. This was the precise instant when history would bend, when the letter would be signed, when the Balfour Declaration would become real.

Chadt raised the envelope. His hands were shaking.

Tian began to speak — words that weren't words, sounds that weren't sounds, a language Chadt's body understood before his mind caught up.

**"We call the name."** Tian's voice resonated in the small room, inaudible to the men but present nonetheless. **"Arthur Balfour. October 31st, 1917. The signatory. The author. The instrument."**

The envelope in Chadt's hands began to glow.

And then everything went wrong.

The Cabinet Room doors slammed open. A figure stood in the doorway — not a servant, not a minister. Something Chadt had never seen before, something that didn't belong in this time or any time. It was made of smoke and mirrors and old, old grief.

Tian went rigid.

"What—" Chadt started.

"Run," Tian whispered. The word was stripped of all composure. "Run now."

But the thing in the doorway was already moving toward them, and Chadt realized with dawning horror that it wasn't chasing them.

It was reaching for the letter in his hands.

The "battle" format is an invention of the Imaginary Plane, not a literal representation of the meeting.
