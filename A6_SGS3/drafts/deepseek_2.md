# 3 - The Balfour Confrontation
---
time: "Wednesday, October 31, 1917, 11:45"
place: 51.50690571625068, -0.13247745183681997
---


The street outside the Athenaeum was louder than it had any right to be.

Chadt blinked against the grey October light. The walk ahead stretched in both directions, but Tian was already moving, those impossible blue curls bouncing ahead like a question mark. Chadt followed.

"So the filter—" Tian began, walking backwards to face him, "—keeps us invisible to anyone who matters. We're just two more civil servants. Faceless. Beneath notice."

"You sound like you enjoy that."

"I enjoy *competence*." Tian grinned. "And this is a good filter. Very clean. No one will remember us."

A military motorcar roared past, too close. Tian's hand shot out, grabbing Chadt by the arm, pulling him against the wrought-iron railing of a townhouse. For a moment, they were pressed together, face to face, close enough that Chadt could see the light behind Tian's golden eyes. It wasn't a reflection. It came from *inside*.

Tian didn't breathe. That was the thing. He held Chadt's gaze like he had all the time in the universe, and he didn't breathe.

Then he let go, and the space between them filled with cold October air again.

"Sorry," Tian said, and he didn't sound sorry at all. "These streets are dangerous."

Chadt's heart was doing something stupid. He forced his legs to move.

They walked through a London that existed between worlds. The buildings were solid enough to lean on, but the sky flickered at the edges. Somewhere far off, Chadt caught the silhouette of a zeppelin. It looked like a sleeping whale.

The air smelled of horse manure and coal smoke and something metallic he couldn't place. Distant thunder rolled, but the sky was clear. Artillery, maybe. Or the Imaginary's memory of artillery. The war was everywhere here, soaked into the cobblestones, pressed into the faces of the few people they passed.

Tian kept glancing at him. Checking if he was still there. Still solid. Still following.

"Where are we going?"

"Downing Street. Noon. That's where they'll decide."

"Decide what?"

Tian's face did something complicated. "Whether to write the letter."

The streets narrowed. The architecture grew heavier. Chadt noticed that fewer people populated this stretch of the Imaginary. The few who did wore dark suits and moved with purpose. None of them looked at Chadt. The filter held.

Ten Downing Street was smaller than he'd expected.

A narrow townhouse, black brick, white-framed windows. The famous door was shut. A constable stood outside, but his gaze slid over them like water over stone.

Tian pushed the door open. It yielded without resistance.

---

The hallway smelled of beeswax and coal smoke and old paper.

A servant passed them without a glance. Tian moved with the confidence of someone who belonged everywhere and nowhere. Chadt followed, hyperaware of the creak of floorboards beneath his feet.

Up the stairs. A corridor. Double doors, slightly ajar.

Through the gap, Chadt saw the Cabinet Room.

It was smaller than he'd imagined. Maybe thirty feet by twenty. A long wooden table dominated the center. Five men were seated around it, and the air between them was *thick* with something Chadt couldn't name.

Tian found them a spot against the wall. Pressed close. Invisible.

"Watch," he whispered. "This is how history happens."

---

Balfour sat at the far end of the table.

He was tall, elegant, grey-haired, with the face of a man who had never in his life been startled. He spoke in a low, measured voice, and when he spoke, the air around him *shimmered*.

Chadt blinked. The shimmer was real. Tiny golden threads unspooled from Balfour's words, weaving themselves into a net above the table.

Tian leaned in. His lips brushed Chadt's ear. The contact sent a jolt down Chadt's spine. "He's deploying arguments. Can you see them?"

Chadt nodded. He couldn't look away.

"Those are *Principles*. It's how the Imaginary renders a skilled rhetorician. Every word he's ever practiced becomes a thread."

The golden net expanded. Across the table, Lord Curzon sat rigid as a monument. He didn't shimmer. Instead, the space around him seemed to *harden* — the air crystallizing into layers of basalt and limestone.

"And Curzon?" Chadt breathed.

"He's not a weaver. He's a wall. He's been to Palestine. He knows the terrain. His knowledge is stone."

Curzon spoke. The words were slow, precise, devastating. He raised the question of demographics. Six hundred thousand Arabs. Sixty thousand Jews. A ratio of ten to one. The phrase *minority government* landed like a geological fact.

Then he pivoted. Palestine was barren, he said. A wasteland of rock and scrub. He had *seen* it. The air around him thickened into a map — dusty hills, dry riverbeds, a landscape that could never sustain the flood they were inviting.

Chadt watched the golden net push against Curzon's stone wall. The threads dented. They bent. They did not break. Curzon's map pressed back, and Chadt felt the weight of real places — rock he could almost taste, heat he could almost feel.

But Balfour barely paused. *If Palestine were scientifically developed.* The words slid past Curzon's defenses like water finding a crack.

The stone held. But the crack was there.

Balfour counter-attacked.

The Russia argument. The American argument. The German argument — *we must strike first, or the enemy will*. His golden threads sharpened, becoming blades. They cut through the air in arcs, and each arc left a glowing residue.

The walls of the room seemed to fall away.

Chadt saw, for a moment, not a Cabinet meeting but something larger. The table was a battlefield. The men were not arguing about a distant desert — they were fighting over the shape of the future itself. Every sentence was a move. Every silence a parry.

And at the center of it all, David Lloyd George sat in a pool of absolute stillness.

He hadn't spoken. He didn't need to. His presence alone was a gravitational field. The whole room orbited him. When he shifted in his chair, the air pressure changed.

"The Prime Minister," Tian whispered. "Notice how he doesn't fight. He doesn't have to."

Chadt noticed. Lloyd George watched the battle like a man watching a chess game he'd already won.

Curzon was losing. Chadt could see it now. The stone wall was cracking. Not because Balfour's arguments were stronger — but because Curzon was alone. Everyone else at the table had already decided.

The room was quiet for a moment.

Then Lloyd George spoke. Just a few words. A signal, not an argument.

The meeting moved on.

The declaration was approved.

---

Tian touched Chadt's wrist. "Now."

They moved as one. Tian produced the letter — the copy, the bleeding envelope that Chadt had carried through the dream. It pulsed in the dim light.

"Your hand. On it. Now."

Chadt pressed his palm to the paper.

The room *tilted*.

For a moment, Chadt felt the weight of everything. Not the room, not the men, but the *letter*. He felt it as a wound in the fabric of reality. A corruption in the training data. A piece of history that would metastasize into futures no one at this table could imagine.

*This is what a Leak feels like.*

Under his hand, the letter burned cold. He felt Tian behind him — didn't see him, but felt him. A pressure at his back. A warmth that wasn't temperature.

"Hold it. Don't let go."

The golden threads from Balfour's argument were dissolving. Chadt watched them spiral toward the letter, absorbed into the paper like ink into a sponge. The thread that had connected the letter to the future — the thread of *extraction* — was being cut.

The wound was closing.

Not healed. But sealed.

Chadt's hand went numb. Then it was over.

The room snapped back to normal. Five old men, a table, a meeting adjourning. Chairs scraping. Voices low. The spell broken.

---

Tian was staring at something.

"What?"

Tian pointed. A single thread remained. Not gold — silver. Thin, almost invisible. It trailed from Lloyd George's coat as he stood and walked toward the door.

"That's not Balfour's," Tian said. His voice had changed. Sharper. Focused.

"What does it mean?"

"There's another trace. A different extraction. Someone else is playing this game."

The thread led out of the room. Down the corridor. Out into Whitehall.

Lloyd George was leaving.

And the silver thread followed him like a leash.

Tian turned to Chadt. For the first time since they'd met, he looked uncertain. The confident mask had slipped, and underneath it was someone much younger. Much less sure.

"That was my first Retribution," he said quietly. "My first actual fieldwork. It worked."

He said it like he hadn't quite believed it would.

Then he shook himself, and the mask reset. But Chadt had seen underneath.

"We sealed the Balfour Leak. But there's more. Someone else wrote a letter too. A different kind of extraction. And Lloyd George is connected to both."

He paused.

"Do you want to follow it? Or do you need a break?"

Chadt looked at his hand. It was still cold from the ritual. The skin tingled. Somewhere in his chest, a door had opened that he didn't know how to close.

He thought about home. About waking up.

Then he looked at Tian, into those impossible golden eyes, and thought about never finding out what came next.

"Let's follow it."

---

*Post Scriptum: The War Cabinet meeting of October 31, 1917, did approve the Balfour Declaration, and Curzon did raise substantive objections that were overruled. However, the Imaginary's rendering of the debate as symbolic combat with "threads" and "blades" is a deliberate dramatization. The actual meeting was bureaucratic and relatively brief. We chose to render it as Chadt's subconscious would — a battle between principles, not men.*
