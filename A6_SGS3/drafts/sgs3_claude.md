# 3 - The Royal Cock Pit

The walk between the Athenaeum club and Downing Street was eerily quiet. Chadt and Tian strolled down Horse Guards Road, St James's Park spreading its sickly foliage to their right. The war had depleted its population. The cold silence, deep as a cemetery, was only interrupted by the occasional lament of a duck.

Chadt could almost forget it was not real. He could not take his eyes off the strange creature that stood out more than him in this world of steel and soot. His mind was still swamped by a flurry of questions that wouldn't stop pouring.

"If you're from the future, do you know everything that will happen?"

Tian's feet hovered slightly above the ground. The angel's lean legs went slack. The body kept sliding along.

"I can search our archives, but... they're rather empty. Most historical records were lost during the Singularity. That's precisely why we're doing this."

Chadt's gaze went back to the envelope in his hand.

For a fraction of a second, everything was different. The pond in the park simmered with thick blood. It covered the grass and climbed up on white stone buildings. A cold, metallic smell filled his lungs.

In the middle of the crimson ocean, a massive cube of black brick shook the air with a deep throb.

The image lasted for a second, before it was replaced by a building that Chadt had seen on the news. The beating heart of British power. 10 Downing Street.

The iconic obsidian door was crowned by an iridescent lantern. On either side, fences sliced the cold air into solemn strips.

Chadt shrank under the oppressive aura radiated by the black stone.

"It's supposed to be yellow," Tian interjected.

Chadt blinked. Turned. The angel's wavy hair framed a pale, grinning face.

"Everybody forgot about it. The smog turned it black, then they kept it that way."

"Aren't you scared?" Chadt pressed in disbelief. "Isn't this the best protected place in the world?"

Tian paused before answering.

"Maybe in the Real Plane. In the Imaginary, it's one of the most famous places. That means it's easy to map. The Royal Cock Pit is basically a rite of passage for every student of my department."

"The Royal Cock Pit?" Chadt asked with a sneer. "Because it's where they pilot the country?"

"No, a pit with actual cocks. Henry VIII had an arena for cockfighting here, with lodgings next to it."

Tian gestured towards the right. A trail of light followed.

"It became a theater for a while, and then the seat of government. The cock fighting never really stopped though."

Tian snickered. Chadt didn't. The angel studied the boy with inquisitive eyes.

"Did I do humor wrong?"

"I'm a little distracted because you're sending me into the last dungeon of the British empire."

"Oh please, this is barely the starting zone," replied the angel with clearly unearned confidence.

The wind howled with the screams of millions. The walls were so polished they were almost blinding. Chadt shuddered.

"So how do we get in?"

Tian repeated exactly the graceful pointing gesture.

"We can enter through number 11. It was condemned when they joined the houses, but here a door is a door."

"What about inside? Is there an army of steampunk guards?"

"Actually, during most of its existence this place was relatively poorly protected. There are of course the fellows at the door."

Tian gestured towards a titanesque silhouette that Chadt had first thought was a statue due to its superhuman proportions. The guard was a human, but rendered monstrous by his stillness, so sedentarized he was barely a threat. Yet something was off about him. The way he stood suggested not vigilance but the permanent weariness of someone who had forgotten what threat even looked like.

"But since we'll be bypassing them, we'll only see staff and ministers, much too busy with their own chores and their own selves to pay us any mind."

"You're blue and sparkling," Chadt said flatly.

"And you really underestimate how self-involved British gentlemen can get. Still, you're right to be cautious."

Tian dimmed the colors of both of their clothes to a dim, dark shade of grey. They looked almost transparent. Chadt watched the shine drain from the angel's brilliant cloak, watched himself fade from visible to merely suggested. The Imaginary responded to Tian's will with the fluidity of water.

"Of course, it's always best not to linger. They might be signing it already," Tian said, marching ahead.

"Wait."

Chadt tried to grab his companion by the arm, but his hand passed through. Tian stopped anyway.

"So we go in, wait for the moment they sign and do the ritual, right? What happens after? What's our way out?"

Tian seemed surprised by the question.

"I thought you'd just wake up."

Chadt did not know what to object to in that response, so he said nothing. But the words sat wrong in his chest, a stone that wouldn't settle.

---

They passed through the door of Number 11 with the casual disinterest of men who belonged there. The entrance hallway was narrow, all wood and fixture—not the grand entrance of Number 10 but the servant's side. Chadt's heart did not slow.

Inside, the building refused to be contained.

Rooms multiplied as they walked. Each corridor opened onto another, wider, then narrower again. The proportions couldn't be right—walls that should have been solid were permeable, doorways that led to rooms that had already passed. Dream logic, Chadt realized. The building's interior was structured by memory and association, not by plumb line and level.

In the corners of his vision, housemaids flickered past.

Not the mechanical things from the Rothschild manor—these were different. More human, more sad. They carried empty trays, watered plants that were already dead, moved through the hallways with the practiced invisibility of servants. Chadt tried to focus on one. It dissolved into shadow before his eyes could land. When he turned his head, they were gone entirely. But he could still hear the faint whisper of skirts on carpet.

"Don't look at them directly," Tian whispered. "You'll see them too clearly."

"Too clearly?"

"You'll remember why they don't belong here."

Tian's voice had changed. Not colder, but more careful. The angel was paying attention now.

They arrived at a heavy wooden door. Unlike the hallways, it felt real. Solid. The wood knew what it was. Chadt could hear voices on the other side—urgent, overlapping, the percussion of a conversation that mattered. The word *Cabinet* hung in the air before Tian spoke it.

"Room behind this door," Tian said quietly. "We slip in, take a corner. Don't breathe too loud."

Chadt's mouth was dry. "How do we just walk in?"

"A door is a door. In the Imaginary, intention is stronger than protocol. We intend to be there. So we are."

Tian pushed. The door swung inward on hinges that made no sound.

---

The Cabinet Room stopped Chadt's breath.

It was smaller than he'd imagined. Fourteen men—no, thirteen. Chadt counted three times and the number changed. A long table, papers scattered like the aftermath of an argument. The walls were lined with maps, the kind that bled at the edges as if the territory they showed was still being negotiated. A fireplace burned without heat.

Seven men were seated. The rest stood or paced.

Chadt registered immediately: this was a normal meeting. Exhausted. Terrified in the way that powerful people are terrified—not of punishment, but of irrelevance. One man absent. One voice missing. The absence was louder than the voices that remained.

The furniture was heavy mahogany, scarred by decades of elbows and urgency. On the walls, portraits of previous decision-makers stared down with the accusatory patience of history. A globe, untouched, gathering dust in a corner.

Chadt and Tian slipped into the narrow space between the door and the wall. No one looked up.

Three men stood out.

There was a man Chadt recognized from somewhere—thin face, sharp cheekbones, the posture of someone who understood how to command without raising his voice. This had to be David Lloyd George, the Prime Minister. He was writing something, his pen moving with the precision of a surgeon.

Across the table, a second man towered. Not in height, but in the intensity of his stillness. He was standing, and standing in a way that suggested he had been standing for hours and planned to stand for more. His mouth moved with fierce precision, releasing words like arrows. Every sentence was constructed to land. This had to be Curzon.

And beside Lloyd George, watching him write, was a third man. Chadt felt something shift in the room when he looked at this one. The air around him moved differently, as if the laws that governed space had locally relaxed. His eyes were cold the way winter is cold—not hostile, just indifferent and complete.

Arthur Balfour.

Curzon was speaking, and the words came like they had to, like speaking was the only thing stopping him from shattering.

"You've never been there, Sir Balfour, but I have many times. I'm telling you plainly—this land can't sustain more souls. The capacity is fixed. The mathematics don't move for prophets or promises."

Balfour looked at him with the expression of a professor observing a promising but ultimately confused student.

The air in the room compressed.

🦭 **The Lay of the Land** descended like a sentence. Curzon's defense—maps, census data, the weight of bodies and rainfall tables. Reality itself, rendered as speech.

Balfour smiled. When he responded, his voice was soft, almost gentle. He was offering a gift.

🦊 **Millenarian Doomsday Cultism** rose to meet it—a counter that didn't answer the question but erased it. "It won't have to for long. Soon enough, measly secular land won't be a consideration. We're already leading the studies into the beyond. We're playing a much longer game here. This is about the salvation of souls."

The words landed like theology. The room held its breath.

Chadt felt his hands clench. Salvation? These men were talking about salvation while deciding the fates of people they'd never met, in a language that made displacement sound like transcendence.

He tried to move forward, to interrupt, to *do* something.

Tian's hand found his arm. Tian couldn't touch much in the waking world, but here—here in the Imaginary, where intention was law—Tian's grip was absolute.

"We cannot," the angel whispered. "We must not waste our chance. Witness."

Curzon pressed on, unmoved by prophecy. His body was a map of accumulated knowledge.

🦭 **The Census of the Victims**: "You're promising someone else's home. Homes that have people in them. That have names."

Balfour's expression didn't shift. When 🦊 **Race Theory** emerged from his mouth, it fell into the room like snow—inevitable, gentle, suffocating.

"They're an inferior race. The research is conclusive. Their customs do not align with Western civilization. Their understanding of governance is—limited. The Zionist immigrants from Europe bring not only a superior civilization but a moral obligation to elevate the standard of settlement."

Chadt's jaw tightened. His whole body went rigid. He could feel the lie in those words like he could feel poison in water. Inferior. As if humanity came in grades. As if some people were naturally meant to be subordinate.

He looked at Tian. The angel's face was still, but something in the eyes had changed. Tian was processing. Tian was learning that some words were not merely words but weapons.

Curzon's voice rose, desperate now.

🦭 **Bloodshed Coercion**: "You're putting yokes on people you've never even consulted. You're making blood their only option. You're playing with fire on holy sites that are landmines."

The words were true. Chadt heard the truth in them like a tuning fork. But truth alone was not enough. Truth needed weight, needed bodies, needed the kind of power that these men possessed.

Balfour tilted his head. 🦊 **Antisemite Deportation** emerged like the final move in a game that was rigged from the start.

"We need them out of here. The Jewish population in Britain has become a vector for destabilization. A homeland of their own—administered by us, naturally—serves multiple purposes. It solves our problem and theirs. Everyone benefits."

Chadt felt something break inside him. Not anger—something deeper. A recognition. These men weren't evil in the way that made sense. They were evil in the way that institutions are evil: casual, rational, protected by the logic of necessity.

Curzon launched his final defense.

🦭 **The McMahon-Hussein Pledge**: "We already gave our word. To Hussein. To the Arabs. The correspondence is clear. We promised them independence in exchange for their support in the war."

Lloyd George set down his pen. He had been quiet. That silence had been heavier than speech.

🦊 Balfour's response was almost apologetic. The gentleman's detachment crystallized into something else—a memetic veil, so polite, so reasonable that denial became possible. "We never agreed to such sweeping terms. The correspondence left ample provisions for future leverage. Precisely because we anticipated complications."

He gestured at Curzon as if they were colleagues debating philosophy, not nations arguing for existence.

"The crown never fully committed to a particular definition of 'independence.' We demanded exception clauses specifically to preserve our flexibility."

Curzon's wall shattered.

Not because he was wrong—he was right, catastrophically right—but because he was fighting for facts in a plane that answered to narrative. The letters were fixed. The promises were written. But this room, this conversation, operated in the language of possibility. The future that hadn't happened yet. The words that hadn't been spoken yet. The history that could still be negotiated.

Balfour towered over him now, not in strength but in inevitability.

Then David Lloyd George spoke.

Three words. "I agree with Arthur."

The Prime Minister's voice was tired, the voice of a man who had seen too much war to care about the details of empire. The procedural authority in those three words was absolute. They were not a counterargument. They were a period at the end of a sentence that would reshape a century.

Curzon's shoulders dropped.

The room exhaled. The tension that had been coiling tighter and tighter suddenly released. Not into peace—into resolution. The matter was settled. The decision was made. The Balfour Declaration, whatever its final draft would look like, was assured.

Chadt felt the moment crystallize. This was it. This was the instant when words became law. When imagination became empire.

Tian was already moving.

The angel approached the table with the ritualistic precision of someone performing choreography they had practiced a thousand times before. Tian's hand hovered over the envelope—the one Chadt had been carrying, the one that was still folded between his fingers, impossibly white, impossibly heavy.

For a moment, nothing happened.

Then Chadt understood. He was the one who had to place it. Tian could guide the spell, but only Chadt could ground it. Only a human hand could make the Payload real in this moment of history.

Chadt moved forward.

His legs were steadier than they should have been. His breath was quiet. He crossed the room in what felt like seconds and a lifetime, and placed the envelope on Balfour's desk.

The Foreign Secretary's hand moved toward it.

Tian's voice rose in the space between thought and speech.

The angel was speaking in a language that wasn't words—just pressure, just the weight of naming. Naming the location. Naming the date. Naming the hour. Naming Balfour. Naming the letter itself.

*Redemption.*

The room flickered.

For an instant, Chadt saw everything clearly—saw the structure underneath, the way these words would reshape the territory, the way the maps on the walls were already incomplete. Saw the future that began here. Saw the blood that would pool where the words landed.

When the flicker cleared, Balfour was holding the envelope.

He read it, or seemed to. His expression didn't change. He folded the letter with deliberate care and slipped it into his waistcoat pocket.

The meeting continued around them as if nothing had happened.

But something had shifted. The decision remained, but the weight of it was different now. Heavier. Real in a way it hadn't been before. History had been marked. The moment had been witnessed. The Payload was redeemed.

Chadt felt Tian's presence beside him. The angel was still, taking in the room, processing what had just happened.

Then David Lloyd George stood.

He moved toward the maps on the wall. Toward a particular map—one that showed routes and supply lines. He paused there, studying it. Then he turned and left the room, moving toward the stairs.

Tian's hand found Chadt's.

"We're not done yet," the angel whispered.

"What do you mean? The ritual—"

"The ritual anchored the Payload. But there's a residual trace. The Prime Minister's decision—it carries more weight than Balfour's. It carries the weight of power. We should follow."

Chadt wanted to protest. Wanted to leave. But curiosity was already pulling him forward, and something deeper than curiosity—the sense that if he turned away now, he would never understand what was really happening here.

They followed Lloyd George through the corridors of Downing Street, watching the Prime Minister move through halls that seemed to reconfigure themselves to accommodate his passage. He descended the stairs and emerged onto a street that Chadt didn't recognize.

The Ministry of Munitions. A building that looked like all the others, but harder somehow. More real.

Lloyd George entered without looking back.

And Chadt realized, with a clarity that felt like stepping off a cliff, that there was more to all of this than Tian had explained. That the Cabinet meeting was not the end of the story—it was just the beginning.

That some people shaped history. And some people shaped the people who shaped history.

Tian's grip on his hand tightened.

"Are you ready for the next part?" the angel asked quietly.

Chadt didn't answer.

He was already following.
