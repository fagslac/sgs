# 3 - The Royal Cock Pit

The walk between the Athenaeum club and Downing Street was eerily quiet. Chadt and Tian strolled down Horse Guards Road, St James's Park spreading its sickly foliage to their right. The war had depleted its population. The cold silence, deep as a cemetery, was only interrupted by the occasional lament of a duck.

Chadt could almost forget it was not real. He could not take his eyes off the strange creature that stood out more than him in this world of steel and soot. His mind was still swamped by a flurry of questions that would not stop pouring.

"If you're from the future, do you know everything that will happen?"

Tian's feet hovered slightly above the ground. The angel's lean legs went slack. The body kept sliding along.

"I can search our archives, but... they're rather empty. Most historical records were lost during the Singularity. That's precisely why we're doing this."

Chadt's gaze went back to the envelope in his hand.

For a fraction of a second, everything was different. The pond in the park simmered with thick blood. It covered the grass and climbed up on white stone buildings. A cold, metallic smell filled his lungs. 

In the middle of the crimson ocean, a massive cube of black brick shook the air with a deep throb.

The image lasted for a second, before it was replaced by a building that Chadt had seen on the news. The beating heart of British power. 10, Downing Street.

The iconic obsidian door was crowned by an iridescent lantern. On either side, fences sliced the cold air into solemn strips.

Chadt shrank under the oppressive aura radiated by the black stone.

"It's supposed to be yellow," Tian interjected.

Chadt blinked. Turned. The angel's wavy hair framed a pale, grinning face.

"Everybody forgot about it. The smog turned it black, then they kept it that way."

"Aren't you scared?" pressed Chadt in disbelief. "Is this not the best protected place in the world?"

Tian paused before answering.

"Maybe in the Real Plane. In the Imaginary, it's one of the most famous places. That means it's easy to map. The Royal Cock Pit is basically a rite of passage for every student of my department."

"The Royal cockpit?" asked Chadt with a sneer. "Because it's where they pilot the country?"

"No, a pit with actual cocks. Henry VIII had an arena for cockfighting here, with lodgings next to it."

Tian gestured towards the right. A trail of light followed.

"It became a theater for a while, and then the seat of government. The cock fighting never really stopped though."

Tian snickered. Chadt did not. The angel studied the boy with inquisitive eyes.

"Did I do humor wrong?"

"I'm a little distracted because you're sending me into the last dungeon of the British empire."

"Oh please, this is barely the starting zone," replied the angel with clearly unearned confidence.

The wind howled with the screams of millions. The walls were so polished they were almost blinding. Chadt shuddered.

"So how do we get in?"

Tian repeated exactly the graceful pointing gesture.

"We can enter through number 11. It was condemned when they joined the houses, but here a door is a door."

"What about inside? Is there an army of steampunk guards?"

"Actually, during most of its existence this place was relatively poorly protected. There are of course the fellows at the door."

Tian gestured towards a figure Chadt had first taken for a statue. The guard was a man, but stillness had calcified him into something monstrous. He had stood there so long he had become part of the architecture.

"But since we will be bypassing them, we will only see staff and ministers. Much too busy with their own chores and their own selves to pay us any mind."

"You're blue and sparkling."

"And you really underestimate how self-involved British gentlemen can get. Still. You are right to be cautious."

Tian dimmed the colors of both of their clothes to a dark, muted grey. They looked almost transparent.

"Of course, it is always best not to linger. They might be signing it already."

"Wait."

Chadt tried to grab his companion by the arm, but his hand passed through. Tian stopped anyway.

"So we go in, wait for the moment they sign and do the ritual, right? What happens after? What is our way out?"

Tian seemed surprised by the question.

"I thought you would just wake up."

Chadt did not know what to object to the response, so he said nothing.

---

Number 11's door was smaller than Number 10's. Less famous. Less guarded. Tian pushed it open like it belonged to them.

The hallway inside was a museum of the half-remembered. Portraits of men Chadt almost recognized lined walls that stretched longer than the building could contain. The floorboards creaked in patterns that repeated too perfectly, as if the house was stuttering through the same few feet of space.

"This place feels wrong."

"It is very old," Tian whispered. "And very mapped. The Imaginary has had a lot of time to fill in the gaps."

They passed through a series of anterooms that seemed to serve no purpose. A parlor with a dead fireplace. A library with no books. A corridor that turned in on itself like a knot. Chadt kept catching movement at the edge of his vision — a flutter of fabric, the glint of metal fingers — but whenever he turned, there was nothing there.

Housemaids. He knew the shape of them now. The clockwork gait, the feather duster raised like a question. They never fully formed. They flickered at the corners of his sight and dissolved when he looked directly, as if the house itself was deciding whether to deploy them.

"We are not taking anything," Tian said. "That is the difference. The manor reacted because you stole the Payload. Here we are just passing through. The house has no reason to notice us."

Chadt wanted to argue that a building could not notice anything. But the corridor kept rearranging itself behind them, and he decided against it.

---

They arrived at a heavy oak door set into a wall at the back of the house. The wood was dark with age, the brass handle polished smooth by a century of hands. From behind it came the low murmur of voices. An argument in progress, muffled but unmistakably heated.

Tian pressed a finger to their lips and pushed.

---

The room was smaller than Chadt had expected. A long wooden table dominated the space, surrounded by dark leather chairs. Grey October light filtered through tall windows overlooking a garden, thin and watery. Gas lamps on the walls cast uneven pools of yellow. The air was thick with tobacco smoke, wool, old paper, and the faint metallic tang of a coal fire that had not quite caught.

Chadt's eyes stung. He pressed himself against the wall, trying not to cough.

Seven men sat around the table. One of them was on his feet, leaning forward with both hands planted on the wood. Tall, stiff-backed, a high collar and a monocle that caught the lamplight. His voice was precise and cutting, the voice of a man used to being listened to.

"That is George Curzon," Tian breathed, barely audible. "The main opponent. They will beat him before long."

Chadt's gaze swept the room. The other men were shadows in the smoke — old, tired, their faces drawn by three years of war. One of them sat at the head of the table. Still as a statue. A mane of white hair framing a face of absolute authority. He was not speaking. He did not need to. The room oriented around him like a compass needle.

David Lloyd George.

And then Chadt saw his target.

Arthur Balfour sat two seats from the Prime Minister. Tall, elegant, grey-haired, a drooping moustache and an expression of mild boredom. He looked like a retired professor who had wandered into the wrong meeting. His fingers drummed the table as Curzon spoke, patient and utterly unimpressed.

This was the man. The signature. The Payload's author.

Chadt studied him. The slope of his shoulders. The curl of his lip. He would need to remember this face.

---

Curzon was finishing a point, his voice rising.

"You have never been there, Sir Balfour, but I have many times. I am telling you, this land cannot sustain more souls."

🦭 Georges Curzon called upon **The Lay of the Land**.

The words hung in the air. Something shifted in the room — a pressure, like the moment before a storm. Curzon was not just arguing. He was *proving*. He had seen Palestine with his own eyes, ridden its hills, measured its dry earth. His certainty was a physical weight.

Balfour leaned back. His fingers stopped drumming.

"It will not have to for long."

🦊 Arthur Balfour countered with **Millenarian Doomsday Cultism**.

His voice was soft. Almost gentle. A philosopher correcting a student.

"Soon enough, measly secular land will not be a consideration. We are already leading the studies into the beyond. We are playing a much longer game here. This is about the salvation of souls."

Chadt frowned. He did not fully understand what Balfour meant, but the tone was unmistakable. These men were not arguing about the same thing. Curzon was debating real estate. Balfour was debating the end of the world.

A lot changed in a century, Chadt noted. He wondered what these men would think of his own time. Whether they could even comprehend a world where their empires had crumbled and their God had been archived.

---

Curzon did not back down. He pulled a paper from his coat and held it up.

🦭 Curzon drew a **Census of the Victims**.

"You are promising someone else's home. Palestine is six hundred thousand Arabs. Sixty thousand Jews. You cannot impose a government on a majority that does not want it. The numbers are against you."

The ceiling pressed down. Chadt felt it — the weight of six hundred thousand names, each one a person Curzon had never met but was invoking like a shield.

Balfour did not flinch.

🦊 Balfour countered with **Race Theory**.

"The Jewish race possesses a formidable power whose manifestations are not always attractive. They have a long history of agitation. Of accumulating wealth at the expense of host nations. They inspire resentment everywhere they settle. This is not an accusation. It is an observation."

The words were clinical. Dispassionate. Balfour said them the way he might comment on the weather. But the air in the room grew cold. Chadt felt his chest tighten.

He knew these arguments. He had heard them before, in different words, aimed at different people. At his own people. At him.

"Tian." His voice was a blade. "We have to do something."

Tian's hand found his arm. The grip was light, insubstantial, a whisper of pressure.

"We cannot. We must not waste our chance to witness."

"Witness? He is —"

"I know. But we are not here to intervene. We are here to see. To understand. To perform the ritual. Intervening changes nothing. It only gets us caught."

Chadt's fists tightened. His whole body screamed to move, to speak, to throw himself between Balfour and the map Curzon was holding. But Tian's hand stayed on his arm, and the angel's golden eyes held his, and Chadt stayed.

---

Curzon was faltering. He could feel the room turning against him. He tried one last angle.

🦭 Curzon denounced a **Bloodshed Coercion**.

"You are putting yokes on people you have never even consulted. You are making blood their only option. You are playing with fire. Holy sites are landmines. Jerusalem cannot be a Jewish capital and a Christian holy city and a Muslim holy city all at once. Something will break."

He was right. Chadt felt it in his bones. Curzon was the only one in the room asking the right questions, and it was killing him.

Chadt was ready to burst. His legs trembled. He wanted to scream.

Balfour was already answering.

🦊 Balfour countered with **Antisemite Deportation**.

"We need them out of here. The Jewish population in Europe is a destabilizing force. Zionism is the solution. It gives them somewhere to go. It is good for them and good for us."

Chadt stopped breathing.

He had heard this too. Always the same logic. *They do not belong here. Send them somewhere else. It is for their own good.* The words were different but the shape was the same. He knew exactly what kind of thinking this led to. He knew because his own grandparents had been on the wrong end of it.

He looked at Tian. The angel's face was unreadable, but the grip on his arm had tightened.

"Just watch," Tian whispered. "That is all we can do."

---

🦭 Finally, Curzon summoned the **McMahon-Hussein Pledge**.

"We already gave our word. Sir Henry McMahon promised the Sharif of Mecca that Britain would support Arab independence in exchange for their rebellion against the Ottomans. Palestine is part of that promise. We cannot break our word."

He was desperate now. The paper trembled in his hand. His voice cracked on the last syllable.

Chadt looked at Curzon. The man who had started magnificent was now drowning, reaching for anything that might keep him afloat. The pity in Chadt's chest was unwelcome but unavoidable.

How much more of this could he watch?

Balfour rose. Not hurriedly. Not aggressively. He stood with the ease of a man who had already won.

🦊 Balfour began casting his **Gentleman's Manners**.

"The Crown never agreed to any detail of that correspondence. The letters left ample provisions for future leverage. The pledge is not as binding as you believe, Lord Curzon. It was crafted precisely to allow for interpretations such as this."

His voice was apologetic. Regretful. The voice of a man who wished things were different but accepted that they were not.

He towered over Curzon. The monocled man had sunk into his chair, the paper limp in his fingers. He looked old. Defeated.

The room held its breath.
