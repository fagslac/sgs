# Ch. 3 — The Balfour Confrontation

51.50335837602891, -0.1275331734210159
Wednesday, October 31, 1917, 11:45

---

The walk from the Athenaeum to Downing Street was short. The streets were grey with coal smoke and the clatter of hooves. Chadt followed Tian through the fog, trying to keep up.

They passed a line of sandwich-board men walking the pavement. One read WAR IS MURDER. Another demanded bread for the poor. Chadt tried to read them all, but Tian moved fast.

"You'll get used to the signs," Tian said over his shoulder.

"I won't."

"You will. That's the problem."

Downing Street was barricaded. A lone policeman stood at the entrance, looking bored. Tian walked straight past him. The man's eyes slid over them like water over stone.

"The filter?" Chadt whispered.

"Junior clerks," Tian said. "We look boring. That's the trick."

Number 10 was smaller than Chadt had expected. A modest black door, a single white step, a lion-headed knocker. A servant opened it before they could knock, saw two young men in civil servant clothes, and stepped aside.

They were in.

The hallway was narrow. Dark portraits lined the walls — dead men in wigs, watching. A staircase curved upward. A corridor stretched left. Tian turned right without hesitation, through a green baize door, down a short passage, then stopped.

"Through here," he said. "Don't speak. Don't draw attention. Stand behind me and watch."

He pushed open a door.

---

The room was small. Thirty feet by twenty. A long wooden table dominated the centre.

The air was thick with tobacco smoke — so dense it stung Chadt's eyes, curled around the gas lamps like fog around streetlights, painted the walls in layers of grey-blue haze.

A fire crackled in a marble hearth. Shadows danced across the ceiling like something alive.

Seated around the table, six men who looked like they carried the weight of a dying world on their shoulders. They did not move like normal people. They sat like statues. Like pieces on a board.

And one of them was glowing.

---

Chadt blinked.

The grey-haired man at the side of the table — elegant, bored-looking, fingers drumming slow against the wood — shimmered at the edges. Lines of faint gold traced his silhouette, flickering like heat haze, like a television tuned to a dead channel. The air around him *bent*, just slightly, the way light bends around a black hole.

"What is that?" Chadt breathed.

Tian's voice came back, barely audible: "That's Arthur Balfour. He's already started."

"Started what?"

"The fight."

---

The monocled man across from Balfour was speaking. His voice was precise, cutting, the voice of a man who had spent a lifetime being the smartest person in every room.

"I have prepared a memorandum," Lord Curzon announced, sliding a document across the table. "Titled 'The Future of Palestine.' I believe it raises certain — practical objections."

Balfour did not look at the document. He looked at Curzon. And smiled.

"I'm sure it does, George."

---

Curzon's eyes narrowed. He *knew* he was being dismissed.

He stood up.

Not dramatically — old men did not rise dramatically. But the air around him shifted. A blue shimmer flickered at his shoulders, cold and sharp. Chadt felt a weight settle in his chest, like being underwater.

**Imperial Authority**, his instincts whispered. **Viceroy's Presence. Rank: Former Head of an Empire.**

"I have *been* to Palestine," Curzon said, and each word landed like a gavel. "I have *walked* its soil. It is a barren wasteland. Water-scarce. Sparsely resourced. It cannot sustain the population you propose to dump on it."

He was building. Chadt could see it now — the blue shimmer condensing around his monocle, his collar, the tips of his fingers. A defensive formation.

"The population today is six hundred thousand Arabs. And sixty thousand Jews. You are promising one people's homeland on another people's land. Have you considered what happens when they resist?"

He thrust a finger at Balfour.

**Skill: The Future of Palestine. Type: Premonition. Status: Deploying.**

The word *RESISTANCE* flickered in the smoke between them, visible for half a second before dissolving.

---

Balfour did not flinch.

He simply *shifted* in his chair. His gold aura flared once, briefly, like a lion waking.

And then he spoke.

"Lord Curzon."

His voice was calm. Unhurried. Almost kind.

"You raise valid points about terrain. About demographics. About practical difficulties."

He paused. The gold light behind him deepened, sharpened.

"But consider the alternative. Consider what happens if we do *nothing*. The Jews of Europe — rootless, stateless, a people without a home — they drift. They accumulate. They become... conspicuous. And when they become conspicuous, people notice. And when people notice, they resent."

He let that hang in the air.

"A national home in Palestine solves the problem. For everyone. The Jews have somewhere to go. The nations of Europe have somewhere to send them. Everyone wins."

He said it like it was the most natural thing in the world. Like he was solving a logistics problem. A shipment of grain. A railway route.

Chadt felt something cold settle in his stomach. This was not cruelty. This was worse.

This was a man who looked at an entire people and saw a *nuisance* to be relocated.

---

**Skill Activation: A Defence of Philosophic Doubt.**
**Type: Invalidation.**
**Effect: All opposing arguments lose structural integrity.**

The gold light around Balfour *pulsed*.

Curzon's blue barrier cracked — a spiderweb of fractures spreading from where Balfour's words had struck it.

"Russia is collapsing," Balfour continued, each word a hammer blow. "The Americans have not yet arrived. German intelligence is preparing its own declaration — offering the Jews exactly what we are considering. If we do not act now, we lose the war. Is that what you want, George?"

**Damage: Moderate.**
**Curzon's HP: 68% → 41%.**

"I want—" Curzon started.

"You want to be right," Balfour said smoothly. "And you *are* right. Palestine is barren. The demographics are unfavourable. The practical difficulties are enormous."

He stood.

The room *tilted*.

**Skill Evolution: Theism and Humanism.**
**Type: Absolute Declaration.**
**Effect: The speaker's position becomes self-evident. Cannot be rationally opposed.**

"*But the war does not care about practical difficulties.*"

Curzon's barrier *shattered*.

The blue light dissolved into sparks, scattering across the table like dying fireflies. The monocled man staggered — not physically, but Chadt felt it, felt the *defeat* ripple through the air around him.

---

And then the white-maned man spoke.

David Lloyd George. The Prime Minister. He had been watching the entire exchange with the expression of a man observing a particularly boring cricket match. He had not moved. He had not interrupted.

He simply said:

"I agree with Arthur."

Three words. Quiet. Final.

And the battle was over.

Curzon's remaining aura — already shattered — collapsed entirely. He sat back in his chair, deflated, and said nothing. The other men murmured their assent. The declaration was approved.

But Chadt was not looking at Curzon.

He was looking at Lloyd George.

Because when the old Prime Minister had spoken, Chadt had seen it. A flicker of something *darker* behind his eyes. A shadow that moved wrong.

This man had not won a debate.

He had *ended one*.

---

The meeting broke up. Men stood, gathered papers, reached for coats. The smoke began to clear.

Chadt felt Tian's hand on his arm.

"Montagu," Tian whispered. "Edwin Montagu. The only Jew in Cabinet. He opposed the declaration."

"What happened to him?"

"Lloyd George sent him to India. 'Your expertise is needed in Bombay.' Officially."

Chadt looked at Lloyd George again. The old man was laughing at something an aide had said. He looked grandfatherly. Warm. Utterly harmless.

He had never been more afraid of anyone in his life.

---

"Now," Tian said. "While they're still here."

The ritual.

Chadt pulled the letter-copy from his pocket. The image-capture from November 2nd. It pulsed faintly in his hand — a heartbeat of leaking Value, dark blood beading at the edges.

Tian placed his hand over Chadt's. Warm. Electric.

"Say it with me."

They spoke together, voices barely audible:

**"Arthur Balfour. Foreign Secretary. Tenth Downing Street. London. The thirty-first of October, nineteen-seventeen. The Balfour Declaration."**

The letter-copy grew hot. A low hum filled Chadt's chest, vibrating through his ribs, his teeth, his skull.

**"We seal this Leak. We name this moment. We plant this flag."**

**"Let it hold."**

The hum became a vibration. The vibration became a *snap* — like a thread breaking, like a bone setting, like a door closing in a house far away.

The blood on the letter evaporated. The smell of copper faded.

The Leak was sealed.

Chadt exhaled. He hadn't realised he'd been holding his breath.

"Is it done?"

Tian nodded slowly. But his eyes were not on the letter.

They were fixed on the chair where Lloyd George had been sitting. A faint trail of shadow clung to it — a residue, barely visible, like smoke from a dying candle.

"Mostly."

---

Outside, the fog had thickened. The street was a blur of gaslight and shadows. Chadt's breath misted in the cold.

He looked at his hands. The letter-copy was ordinary paper now. No blood. No pulse. Just ink and history.

"She's gone," he said. "The Leak."

"Yes." Tian was already walking, his blue hair catching the light of a passing motorcar. "One down."

"Then why do you look like we lost?"

Tian stopped. Turned. His golden eyes were flat, unreadable.

"Because Balfour was just the instrument. Lloyd George is the architect. And that trail on his chair?"

He pointed east, toward a part of London Chadt didn't recognise.

"Ministry of Munitions. Where the chemists work."

Chadt didn't understand. But he understood Tian's face.

They were not done.

---

He started walking again.

Chadt followed.

Behind them, Number 10 Downing Street sat quiet in the grey afternoon. Inside, Balfour was reaching for his coat. Curzon was gathering his papers with deliberate slowness, his monocle catching the firelight one last time.

None of them noticed the two boys who had stood at the back of the room.

None of them knew that their words had been witnessed.

None of them knew that history was a wound, and someone was trying to close it.
