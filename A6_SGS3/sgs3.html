    3 - The Royal Cock Pit

    The walk between the Athenaeum club and Downing Street was eerily quiet. Chadt and Tian strolled down Horse Guards Road, St James's Park spreading its sickly foliage to their right. The war had depleted its population. The cold silence, deep as a cemetery, was only interrupted by the occasional lament of a duck.

    Chadt could almost forget it was not real. He could not take his eyes off the strange creature that stood out more than him in this world of steel and soot. His mind was still swamped by questions that wouldn't stop pouring.

    "If you're from the future, do you know everything that will happen?"

    Tian's feet hovered slightly above the ground. The angel's lean legs went slack. The body kept sliding along.

    "I can search our archives, but... they're rather empty. Most historical records were lost during the Singularity. That's precisely why we're doing this."

    Chadt's gaze went back to the envelope in his hand.

    For a fraction of a second, everything was different. The pond in the park simmered with thick blood. It covered the grass and climbed up on white stone buildings. A cold, metallic smell filled his lungs. Chadt's head was spinning.

    In the middle of the crimson ocean, a massive black cube shook the air with a deep throb.

    The image lasted for a second, then it was gone, replaced by a building that Chadt recognized from the news. The beating heart of British power. 10, Downing Street.

    The iconic obsidian door was crowned by an iridescent lantern. On either side, fences sliced the cold air into solemn strips. Chadt shrank under the building's oppressive aura.

    "It's supposed to be yellow," Tian interjected.

    Chadt blinked. Turned. The angel's wavy hair framed a pale, grinning face.

    "Everybody forgot about it. The smog turned it black, then they kept it that way."

    "Aren't you scared? pushed Chadt in disbelief. Isn't this the best protected place in the world?"

    Tian paused before answering.

    "Maybe in the Real Plane. But it's also very famous, which means we know it really well. Everything always leads back here. The Royal Cock Pit is basically a rite of passage for every student of my department."

    "The Royal Cockpit? asked Chadt with a sneer. Do they call it that because it's where they pilot the country?"

    "No, we call it that because it was a pit with actual poultry. Henry VIII built an arena for cockfighting right there, with guest lodgings next to it."

    Tian gestured towards the right. A trail of light followed.

    "It became a theater for a while, and then the seat of government. The cock fighting never really stopped though."

    Tian snickered. Chadt didn't. The angel studied the boy with inquisitive eyes.

    "Did I do humor wrong?"

    "I'm a little distracted because you're sending me into the last dungeon of the British empire," snapped Chadt.

    "Oh please," replied the angel with clearly unearned confidence. "This is barely the starting zone."

    The wind howled with the screams of millions. Chadt shuddered. The sun reflecting off the overly polished walls blinded him.

    "So how do we get in?"

    Tian repeated exactly the graceful pointing gesture.

    "We can enter through number 11. It was condemned when they joined the houses, but here a door is a door."

    "What about the security? Is there an army of steampunk guards?"

    "Actually, during most of its existence this place was relatively poorly protected. There are of course the fellows at the door."

    Tian gestured towards two black shapes that Chadt had not noticed. The guards seemed to have sedentarized into the wall. Their proportions far exceeded those of normal humans, and their skin resembled stone. They did not seem to breathe.

    "But since we'll be bypassing them, we'll only see staff and ministers, much too busy with their own chores and their own selves to pay us any mind."

    "You're blue and sparkling," observed Chadt dubiously.

    "And you really underestimate how self-involved British gentlemen can get. Still, you're right to be cautious."

    Tian seemed absent for a while. Then the colors of both of their clothes faded to dim, dark shade of grey. They looked almost transparent.

    "I weakened our signal, that should be enough," Tian explained, marching ahead. "Of course, the best way to not get in trouble is to minimize our exposure time. Let's go, they might be signing the letter already."

    "Wait!"

    Chadt tried to grab his companion by the arm, but his hand passed through. Tian stopped anyway.

    "What happens after the ritual? I mean, what's our way out?"

    Tian seemed surprised by the question.

    "I figured you would just wake up."

    Chadt did not know what to say to that. He supposed this was a valid strategy.

    He ran after Tian, who was already pushing the much smaller, much more innocuous door numbered 11. Chadt stared apprehensively as the stone guardians, but they did not move an inch. They might as well have been standing for centuries.

    The door opened on an antechamber that looked like a museum. The walls were lined with an orderly grid of portraits of old white men in pompous attire that made it hard to tell them apart. The floor was checkered and slippery.

    On every wall, the same arch opened towards a remarkably identical hall. Same yellow walls, same portraits, same arches that continued in a seemingly infinite recursion.

    "The Halls of Power are easier to navigate when you know the way", commented Tian.

    They turned right, then left, then forward for a while. The echoes of their footsteps and the subtle changes in the artworks around them were the only proof that they had moved at all.

    Chadt quickly lost all sense of direction. The cascade of rooms blurred into a repetitive motif he could not grasp. A couple of times, he thought he saw far in the distance a robed silhouette waving too many metallic fingers. But it all went away as soon as he focused his gaze.

    Finally Tian gestured him to stop.

    They had arrived in a house at the back of the building. A few steps ahead, a large white door failed to contain an agitated discussion.

    Chadt's heartbeat accelerated. Cold sweat crept down his spine.

    "Be careful from now on," whispered Tian.

    The advice was, of course, completely unnecessary. The acrid smell of coal smoke smothered them as they took their final careful steps.

    "Look through the keyhole," advised Tian.

    "What about you?"

    "I see what you see."

    Chadt pushed the implications aside, and leaned in with extreme care. The lock seemed to expect an enormous key, so he had no trouble getting a clear view.

    A thin, oval table occupied all the space, surrounded by a battalion of cushioned chairs. In the middle, six gentlemen sat on one side, observing with various stages of boredom another man shuffling through piles of paper.

    "That's Georges Curzon", came the voice of Tian in Chadt's head. It reminded him of the manor escape and sent his heart for another spin.

    "He's the only opposition left. Once he falls, they sign."

    Inside, Curzon called upon the Lay of the Land:

    "You have never been there, Mr. Balfour. I have been, many times. I say this with no pleasure. The water scarce. The land is dry. The population already exceeds what it can carry. It cannot absorb more people."

    His voice was cutting and precise, delivering a verdict, not an argument.

    On the other side of the table, an elderly, languid man listened with amused judgment. His nonchalance contrasted with the impassive detachment of the gentleman sitting in the presiding chair. The other men seemed so uninterested they might have been asleep.

    Once Curzon was done talking, the old man did not answer immediately. The opponents stared at each other, under the icy watch of the Prime Minister.

    When Arthur Balfour spoke, it was with the tone of a patient teacher correcting a child so utterly wrong it was endearing.

    Balfour countered with Psychical Spiritualism:

    "You speak of soil and water, Lord Curzon, as if matter were of any real importance. Man's existence is a brief and transitory episode on one of the meanest of planets. The Earth itself will go tideless and inert."

    Balfour's demeanour dismissed everything else as insignificant. Chadt had seen this arrogance too much in his own time. His fingers pressed deep into the sinuous doorframe.

    "Our measure cannot be the material. I anchor our policy in a moral law, immutable, eternal. In its governance alone do spirits find their true freedom."

    Balfour denied reality with a whimsical smile. Curzon's clean-shaven lips trembled under the blow.

    Chadt held his breath.

    The rigid bureaucrat rummaged hastily through mountains of papers, foraging for something solid to hold on to.

    He finally found it, and brandished a Census of the Victims:

    "You speak of souls, Mr. Balfour. Let me offer a census. Seven hundred thousand Arabs already live there. All accounted for right here. You are promising someone else's home."

    The cold number sank deep in Chadt's chest. It barely made a dent in Balfour's aloofness.

    He smoothed a hand over his oiled hair, tucking a white strand into place. His high-pitched voice was flat when he responded:

    "The weak point of our position, of course, is that we deliberately and rightly decline to accept the principle of self-determination."

    Then the gaslights dimmed, and Chadt's body turned stiff.

    Balfour chuckled and invoked Race Theory:

    "But men are not born equal. I am sorry if that offends the modern sensibility, but it is a fact."

    Heat climbed up Chadt's neck. His nails bit into the painted wood. Balfour smiled as he continued:

    "The white and black races are not born with equal capacities. You cannot build a policy on the fiction that every soul weighs the same."

    The floor began rumbling under Chadt's feet. The politicians did not seem to mind.

    "We have to stop him", Chadt determined.

    "We can't, replied the angel. It has already happened. If they kick us out we'll have to start over."

    The walls fractured. Outside the window, thick black clouds poured a crimson rain.

    "Please," Tian advised. "Stay calm."

    Portraits fell from the walls all around them.

    "Calm? Do you even hear these men? How many hundred thousands lives was it?"

    "I know, whimpered Tian. But there is nothing we can do..."

    On the other side of the door, the British gentlemen carried on, oblivious to the ongoing collapse of their environment.

    Curzon was struggling to maintain composure. The metal corset that usually elevated his presence now made his silhouette look monstrous in the red ambient glow.

    Curzon grabbed something, took a deep breath, and brandished Cascading Violence Predictions:

    "I must insist. All you are doing is provoking bloodshed. And not in Palestine alone."

    Red liquid was seeping through the cracks on the wall. And yet somehow the words kept coming.

    "Every government on the Continent will have the pretext it requires. You will have stamped every Jew as strangers in their own countries. You will have given their persecutors a conscience. Heaven knows they endure enough already."

    Curzon marked a calculated pause for emphasis. He shot a discreet glance at the Prime Minister, who remained as stonefaced as ever. Trapped in their own scene, neither of them noticed blood crawling up their chairs.

    "You are not solving the Jewish question. You're drowning it in never-ending violence!"

    Curzon sank back in his chair, exhausted. The calibrated line failed to elicit any reaction from the slouched old man.

    Around the intruders, the other rooms vanished in a blur of colors. The crimson liquid was pooling at their feet.

    "I want to leave, Chadt begged."

    "You have to stay."

    Arthur Balfour finally leaned forward. He still seemed out of place, like a carefree professor in the middle of serious politicians.

    With a sardonic smile, he revealed his Antisemitic Deportation Aspiration:

    "My dear Lord Curzon, you do dramatise so. It's no different from the position of an Englishman who leaves his country for the United States."

    He punctuated his explanation with a hollow laugh.

    "Except, of course, that in the present position of Jewry, the assimilation was often felt to be incomplete. The Jew remains a people apart, only intermarried among themselves."

    Nausea built up in Chadt. The warm liquid slithered up the skin of his legs.

    "We gain no advantage in harboring people of divided loyalty. Our civilization has too long been tormented by the presence in its midst of a body which it regarded as alien and even hostile, but which it was equally unable to expel or to absorb. Our declaration solves this for good."

    Chadt was suffocating.

    A loud rumble erupted. The room shattered down around them.

    The blood covered Chadt's hands. It pressed upon his chest. It squeezed his throat.

    "We're losing connection", screamed Tian somewhere.

    The last of the ministers drowned.

    Paintings lurched in an infinite red sea.

    "Breathe."

    Chadt couldn't. The crimson liquid was filling his lungs. He tried to rub his hands, but his body did not answer.

    There was no more body, there was only the blood.

    Then, from somewhere, a touch against his hand. Something electric, both cold and warm, impossibly alien. And in front of his face, a reassuring smile, and azure wavy hair.

    "I know it's hard. But we must bear witness."

    The words were soothing. Those that came after had a different ring:

    "You have to see it."

    The turmoil had quieted, but the Imaginary Plane was holding by a thread.

    Chadt and Tian were swimming in a warm liquid, in a sea of splintered artworks. A few steps away, the oval table ebbed with the currents, occasionally blinking out of existence. The white men on their chairs floated around, entirely busy staring at each other.

    For a while, not much moved. The silence was only disturbed by the noise of the waves, the wheezing breath of Curzon, and the snoring of unconcerned politicians.

    The exhausted bureaucrat had shrunk significantly under the sharp gaze of the Prime Minister. He could still reach the table, though.

    He grabbed a final document with a trembling, childlike hand.

    Curzon gathered his strength and recalled the McMahon-Hussein Pledge:

    "Mr Balfour, we agree on more than you think."

    The wheezing voice was barely audible.

    "I concede the paramount strategic importance of securing Jewish goodwill. But you are raising expectations you cannot fulfil, playing with fire on the most dangerous powder keg in the world."

    Curzon swallowed with difficulty. The waves were intensifying, under a glacial howl carrying countless screams.

    "Our priority must be retaining the Christian and Moslem Holy Places in Jerusalem and Bethlehem. We can't alienate our assets in the region. We already purchased them with the same promise!"

    Chadt's heart sank as the waves rose higher. For Curzon too, people were nothing more than chips in a game. They did agree on more than they thought.

    "How are we to keep any control if our word counts for nothing? We are gentlemen, Mr. Balfour. Our word is meant to hold. If it does not, then what are we?"

    For the first time, Balfour seemed taken aback. His face twisted as he tried to recall the precise terms of the McMahon-Hussein Pledge. His chair bucked like a mad beast.

    The dark clouds had come back with thunder and fury. The wind sent sharp splinters lashing everyone's faces.

    "Focus, Chadt, ordered Tian. You must not fail. We're almost there. I don't want to have to try again."

    Something in Chadt snapped. The words. The gap between the words. Between Tian's words. The old men's words. His own words. The same reality they all referred to.

    There was no reality.

    There was only blood.

    Balfour's eyes lit up.

    He rose on his seat.

    Fear flashed in Curzon's eyes.

    The thunder roared.

    Space fractured.

    Chadt screamed.

    Words dissolved.
